What happened, simply

Dad fell back in March and tore his shoulder. On May 11, the surgeon fixed it. Now it has to heal slowly. Your job for the next 6 weeks is to keep his arm still and keep him comfortable.

The fall and the tear

On March 3, Dad fell and landed on his elbow. The force jammed his arm up and ripped the tendons in his shoulder. These tendons are called the rotator cuff. They are what let you lift your arm.

Before the fall, Dad could use his arm normally. After it, he could barely lift it halfway, and it hurt all the time, especially at night. An MRI scan showed the tendons were torn off the bone.

The surgery

On May 11, Dr. Silver fixed the tear. He used a tiny camera and small tools through small cuts in the shoulder. This is much easier than open surgery. It also means smaller scars.

He sewed the torn tendons back to the bone. He used special little anchors to hold them in place. He also added a patch on top, like a bandage on the inside, to make the repair stronger.

The surgery went well. No problems. Dad came home the same day.

The repair held. The tendon was flush against the bone.
From the surgeon's notes

What to know going in

  • The shoulder needs 4 to 6 weeks in the sling. No exceptions.
  • Then physical therapy starts and lasts months.
  • Full recovery takes 6 to 9 months. Be patient.
  • The biggest risk is moving the arm too soon. If the repair fails, he needs another surgery.

The 4 most important rules

  1. Sling stays on. Day and night. Even sleeping. Only off for showers.
  2. No lifting with that arm. Nothing heavier than a coffee cup.
  3. Take pain meds with food. Always. Or he'll get sick to his stomach.
  4. Ice the shoulder. 15 to 20 minutes at a time. Many times a day for the first week.

A normal day, roughly

For the first 2 weeks, every day looks like this. Don't stress about exact times. Just make sure each thing happens.

Morning
  • Help him out of bed slowly
  • Bathroom, then breakfast
  • Take morning meds (with food)
  • Pain meds if he needs them
  • Ice the shoulder, 15-20 min
  • Check fingers look pink
Afternoon
  • Lunch (with food = pain meds OK)
  • Short walk if he's up for it
  • Ice again, 15-20 min
  • Rest in a recliner or propped up
  • Help with anything two-handed
Evening
  • Dinner with evening meds
  • Ice one more time
  • Pain meds if needed
  • Help him brush teeth, wash up
Bedtime
  • Pain meds BEFORE sleep
  • Sling stays ON
  • Pillows around the arm
  • Phone within reach

The sling: how to put it on

  1. Sit him down. Rest his arm in his lap or on a pillow.
  2. Slip his elbow into the bend of the sling.
  3. Reach the long strap up and over the OTHER shoulder (the good one).
  4. Pull the Velcro through the rings on the front, then stick it down.
  5. Check: elbow at 90 degrees, arm across his belly, only wrist and hand showing.

Ice / cooling sleeve

  • 15 to 20 minutes on, then off. Repeat 3-5 times a day in week 1.
  • If he has the cooling sleeve (the machine that pumps cold water), follow the machine's directions. Better than ice packs.
  • For ice packs, wrap in a thin towel. Never put ice straight on skin.
  • Best times: morning, before bed, before pain meds.

Showering

  • First 5 days: no showers. Sponge baths only.
  • After day 5: Take the sling off. He sits on a shower bench or stands carefully. His arm hangs straight down at his side. You wash him. Pat dry. Sling back on right away.
  • No baths, pools, hot tubs until the doctor says OK.

Things he absolutely cannot do

  • Lift anything heavier than a coffee cup with that arm
  • Reach overhead
  • Sleep without the sling
  • Drive while taking oxycodone
  • Drink alcohol while on pain meds
  • Take other pain pills (like Advil or aspirin) without asking the doctor

All the medications

Two new pain pills to start. Some old ones to stop. Everything else stays the same.

The two new pain medications

Strong · Use with care
Oxycodone
also called Roxicodone · 5 mg pill
2 pills every 6 hours, only if needed Drop down to 1 pill as the pain gets better. Never more than 8 pills (40 mg) in one day. Always take with food. The bottle has 30 pills total.
Mild · Safer
Tylenol
also called Acetaminophen · 500 mg pill
2 pills every 6 hours, only if needed Use this in BETWEEN oxycodone doses for better coverage. The bottle has 60 pills.

The pain plan: alternate them every 3 hours

Pain pills wear off after 4-6 hours. If you take them together, there are stretches with no coverage. If you alternate them every 3 hours, something is always working. Always with food.

The first night rule

The numbing block from surgery wears off in the middle of the night. Take pain medication BEFORE bed on the first night. With food. Don't skip this. He'll thank you in the morning.

Pills to STOP taking

These were paused for the surgery. Don't restart any of them without asking the doctor first.

  • Aspirin 81 mg (Bayer)
  • Voltaren 75 mg (also called diclofenac)
  • Gralise 300 mg (also called gabapentin)
  • Crestor 10 mg (also called rosuvastatin)
  • Prilosec 40 mg (also called omeprazole)
  • Flonase nasal spray
  • Kenalog ointment
  • Alpha Lipoic Acid, Vitamin B Complex

Aspirin and Voltaren make you bleed more. They were stopped before surgery. Ask Dr. Silver or Dr. Beker when he can start them again.

Pills to KEEP taking as normal

  • Metformin 500 mg — once a day with meals (for his diabetes)
  • Hyzaar 100/12.5 mg (losartan-HCTZ) — once a day (blood pressure)
  • Toprol XL 25 mg (metoprolol) — once a day (heart/blood pressure)
  • Pletal 100 mg (cilostazol) — twice a day, morning and evening (circulation)
  • Blood sugar checks — same as always

NEVER mix oxycodone with these

  • Alcohol. Even one drink. It can stop his breathing.
  • Sleeping pills (Ambien, etc.)
  • Anti-anxiety pills (Xanax, Ativan, Klonopin)
  • Other pain pills from any source

When to worry

Most of recovery is boring and uneventful. Good. But know what to watch for. These are the things that need a phone call or an ER trip.

Call 911 / Go to the ER NOW

  • Chest pain or trouble breathing
  • His fingers turn pale, blue, or purple
  • His arm stays totally numb after 48 hours
  • Heavy bleeding from the bandage
  • He's confused, very weak, or dizzy
  • He hasn't peed in 8+ hours
  • You can't wake him up or his breathing is very slow

Call Dr. Silver's office: 929-455-2000

  • Fever over 101°F (38.3°C)
  • Chills
  • Cuts get red, hot, swollen, or have pus
  • Pus or strange liquid coming from the cuts
  • Pain that the medication is not helping at all
  • Throwing up that won't stop
  • Pain or swelling in his calf (lower leg) — could be a blood clot

If you can't reach the office and you're worried, take him to urgent care or the ER. Tell them he had shoulder surgery on May 11 with Dr. Silver at NYU.

Things that look bad but are normal

  • Bruising down the arm. Normal. The blood drains down with gravity. Can spread to his hand. Goes away in 1-2 weeks.
  • Some swelling. Normal. Ice helps.
  • Tingling or "pins and needles" as the numbing block wears off. Normal. This is the cue to take pain meds.
  • Constipation. Normal from the pain pills. Push fluids and fiber. A laxative is fine.
  • Trouble sleeping. Common. Sleeping in a recliner helps.
  • Feeling down or grumpy after a few days. Normal. Surgery is hard on the spirit.
